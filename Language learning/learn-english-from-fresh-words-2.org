* controversy

A "*controversy*" is *a prolonged public dispute or debate involving strong, often opposing opinions*. It typically arises when a decision, action, or belief clashes with the values or perspectives of different groups of people, generating widespread discussion or anger.

** Key Characteristics
- *Public in Nature:* Unlike a private argument, a controversy usually plays out in the media, public forums, or between large institutions.
- *Prolonged:* It represents a sustained clash of ideas rather than a single, brief disagreement.
- *Varying Viewpoints:* It features stark divides, where one group supports an idea or action while another strongly objects to it.

** Usage Examples
#+BEGIN_QUOTE
"The proposed policy changes caused a massive *controversy* in the community."
#+END_QUOTE

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
"He resigned from his position amid considerable *controversy*."
#+END_QUOTE

** Common Synonyms
- *Dispute:* An argument or disagreement, especially an official one.
- *Debate:* A formal discussion on a particular topic in which opposing arguments are put forward.
- *Altercation:* A noisy or angry dispute.
- *Polemic:* A strong verbal or written attack on someone else's opinions.

* personnel

"*Personnel*" refers to *the people employed by a company, organization, or military branch*. It is essentially a collective term for a workforce, staff, or team. Additionally, the word is often used to describe *the department that handles hiring and employee management (Human Resources)*.

** Key Usages
*** As a Collective Noun
Refers to the staff.
- Example: /"The new CEO plans to make major changes across all personnel."/

*** As a Department (HR)
Refers to Human Resources.
- Example: /"Please reach out to Personnel to update your tax forms."/

*** By Industry
Commonly paired with specific sectors.
- Examples: 
  - "military personnel"
  - "medical personnel"
  - "security personnel"

** Common Confusion: Personnel vs. Personal
People often confuse these two words because they look and sound similar, but they have completely different meanings.

*** Personnel (Noun)
Defines **Staff/Employees**.
- Example: /"The office personnel attended the annual meeting."/

*** Personal (Adjective)
Defines **Private/Individual**.
- Example: /"These are my personal belongings."/

* resort

A "*resort*" is *a self-contained commercial establishment or holiday town*. It provides a wide variety of amenities, including:
- Accommodations
- Food and dining
- Entertainment
- Recreational activities

Unlike a standard hotel which is primarily for sleeping, a resort serves as a primary vacation destination where all needs are met on-site.

** Grammatical Usages and Meanings
The word can function as both a noun and a verb, with three distinct meanings.

*** Holiday Destination (Noun)
A popular vacation spot designed for relaxation, sport, or leisure.
**** Examples
- Ski resorts in the mountains
- Beach resorts on the coast
- All-inclusive spa resorts

*** A Final Option (Noun)
Used in the phrase "last resort," referring to a final course of action taken only when everything else has failed.
**** Example
"Going to court was a last resort after negotiations broke down."

*** To Turn to for Help (Verb)
When used as "resort to," it means to adopt a specific strategy or action—often something undesirable—because you have no other choice.
**** Example
"They had to resort to legal action to get their deposit back."

* consult

To "*consult*" means *to seek information, advice, or an expert opinion from a person, reference material, or institution before making a decision*. It can also mean to discuss an issue with others or to work professionally as an advisor.

** Common Uses of "Consult"
*** Seeking Advice
Asking a specialist for their professional guidance.
- *Example:* "If your symptoms get worse, you should consult your doctor."

*** Checking Information
Referring to a source or document to find facts.
- *Example:* "If you don't know the definition of a word, consult a dictionary."

*** Discussing/Deliberating
Conferring with others to reach a mutual decision.
- *Example:* "I will need to consult with my colleagues before making a final offer."

** Related Forms
- *Consultant (noun):* An expert who is paid to give professional or technical advice to a business or organization.
- *Consultation (noun):* A meeting with an expert or professional to discuss a specific problem or plan.

* prevail

"*Prevail*" primarily means *to triumph, succeed, or prove superior after a struggle or period of opposition*. It is a versatile verb used to describe winning an argument, overcoming difficulties, or having a specific idea or custom be widely accepted.

** Contextual Usage
The term is commonly used across a few different contexts, including winning in competition or law, having widespread influence, or successfully persuading someone:

*** To Triumph or Win
To emerge victorious in a contest, struggle, or legal dispute.
#+BEGIN_QUOTE
"Despite the challenges, the defense team managed to *prevail* in the high-profile court case."
#+END_QUOTE

*** To be Widespread or Predominant
To be broadly accepted, current, or existing as the most common feature in a certain time or place.
#+BEGIN_QUOTE
"A culture of innovation continues to *prevail* in the technology sector."
#+END_QUOTE

*** To Persist Over Time
To endure or survive.
#+BEGIN_QUOTE
"Let's hope that common sense will *prevail* in the end."
#+END_QUOTE

*** To Persuade (Prevail on/upon)
To successfully convince someone to do something.
#+BEGIN_QUOTE
"We were able to *prevail upon* her to share her expertise at the upcoming workshop."
#+END_QUOTE

* induce

To "*induce*" means *to persuade someone to do something or to cause a specific event or physical condition to happen*. It comes from the Latin /inducere/, meaning "to lead or bring in".

** Key Definitions and Usages
*** To persuade or influence
Persuading or leading someone to take a certain action or position.
- /Example:/ Nothing could induce me to jump out of an airplane.

*** To cause or bring about
Stimulating the occurrence of a physiological or chemical state.
- /Example:/ Medication was used to induce sleep.
- /Note:/ In medicine, it often refers to artificially starting labor in a pregnant woman.

*** In physics and electronics
The process by which an electrical or magnetic field produces an electric current in a nearby object without direct contact.

** Common Synonyms
*** Persuade
- Prompt
- Convince
- Urge
- Influence

*** Cause
- Trigger
- Generate
- Prompt
- Provoke

* shed

The word *shed* is a versatile English word that can function as both a noun (refering to *structure*) and a verb (meaning *to cast off, lose, or release*).

** As a Noun
A *shed* typically refers to *a simple, single-story building used for shelter, storage, or specialized activities*.
- *Examples:* Garden shed, tool shed, lambing shed, factory shed.

** As a Verb
As an action, *shed* means *to cast off, lose, emit, or release*.

*** Discard or Cast Off
Losing a natural covering or getting rid of unwanted things.
- *Animals:* A dog shedding fur or a snake shedding skin.
- *Plants:* A tree shedding leaves in autumn.
- *Idiomatic:* Shedding a few pounds (losing weight) or shedding clothes.

*** Emit or Give Off
Radiating something outward.
- *Example:* A flashlight shedding light on a dark path.

*** Pour or Spill
Letting a liquid flow freely.
- *Examples:* Shedding tears (crying) or shedding blood (injury/war).

* ethic

An "*ethic*" is *a moral principle, a set of values, or a system of rules that guides a person or group on what is right and wrong*.

** Core Definitions
*** Moral Rules
A guiding set of beliefs or standards that controls how people act and treat others.
*** Field of Study
As /ethics/ (plural in form), it is the branch of philosophy that studies human conduct and moral duty.
*** Group Standards
Specific codes used by professions or cultures, such as medical or business ethics.

** Key Variations
*** Personal vs. Professional
An individual has a personal sense of right and wrong, while a job or organization follows a formal code of ethics.
*** Ethics vs. Morals
Morals are personal values, while ethics often refer to the shared rules of a group or community.

* poll

A "*poll*" is *a survey or questionnaire used to gather public opinion, or a formal process of voting to record and count choices or opinions*.

** Common Contexts

*** Public Opinion (Opinion Poll)
An activity where a representative sample of people are asked questions to determine general trends, sentiments, or preferences.
- *Example:* A nationwide *poll* showed 60% of citizens support the new policy.

*** Elections and Voting
The casting, recording, or calculating of votes, or the specific location where voting takes place. In this context, "the polls" usually refers to the physical voting places.
- *Example:* Voters across the country will go to the *polls* on Tuesday.

*** Usage as a Verb
To "poll" means to question people to gather data, or to receive a specific number of votes in an election.
- *Example:* The candidate *polled* over 10,000 votes.

*** Computing
In technology, polling is the process of a computer or system repeatedly and sequentially checking connected devices or channels to see if they are ready for a transmission or to collect data.

* dean

The word "*dean*" primarily refers to *a senior administrative official in an institution*. It carries specific meanings across education, religion, and professional groups.

** Core Meanings
*** In Education
In higher education and some private schools, a dean is an academic leader with significant authority over a specific administrative or academic division.
**** Academic Dean
Heads a specific faculty, school, or college within a university (e.g., Dean of the Faculty of Medicine or Dean of Law).
**** Dean of Students / Student Affairs
An administrator responsible for student life, including counseling, disciplinary issues, and housing.

*** In the Church
The term has historical roots in Christian clergy.
**** Cathedral Dean
The head of a chapter of canons and administrator of a cathedral church.
**** Rural Dean
A priest appointed by a bishop to oversee a specific district within a diocese.

*** As a Professional "Elder" (Doyen)
Outside of academia, "dean" is used to describe the most senior, longest-serving, or most respected person in a specific profession or group.
- /Example:/ "He is the dean of Washington political correspondents."

** Etymology and Names
*** Given Name / Surname
As a name, Dean comes from Old English, originally referring to someone who lived in or near a valley.

* diminish

** Key Definitions & Usage
*** To decrease
To reduce in amount or degree.

#+END_QUOTE

*** To belittle

#+BEGIN_QUOTE

* revise

To "*revise*" means *to re-examine and alter a piece of writing, a plan, or an opinion to correct mistakes, improve accuracy, or reflect new information*. In British English, it also means to study previously learned material to prepare for an examination.

** Key Meanings and Contexts
*** Editing and Correction
To review a manuscript, proposal, or document to improve its quality.

*** Updating Plans or Figures
To adjust forecasts, budgets, or plans based on updated circumstances (e.g., revising a budget upwards).

*** Changing Perspectives
To re-evaluate or reconsider a prior judgment about someone or something.

*** Studying for Exams (UK/Commonwealth)
To review and reread academic notes in preparation for a test.

** Usage Examples
- *Writing:* "The publisher asked the author to *revise* the final chapter before printing".
- *Academics:* "I need to stay home this weekend to *revise* for my history exam".
- *Planning:* "Due to inflation, the project timeline has been *revised*".

** Synonyms
*** Text/Ideas
- Edit
- Amend
- Alter
- Modify
- Rework

*** Studying
- Review
- Cram
- Study

* chronic

The word "*chronic*" generally describes *something that continues for a long time, recurs frequently, or is difficult to eradicate*. It originates from the Greek word /chronos/, meaning "time". 

** Primary Contexts
*** Medicine and Health
In a medical context, *chronic* refers to a disease, condition, or symptom that is persistent, long-lasting (typically lasting 1 year or more), and often develops slowly. 

- *Examples:* Asthma, diabetes, arthritis.
- *Contrast:* It is the opposite of *acute*, which describes a sudden, severe onset of short duration.

*** General Usage
Outside of healthcare, the word describes habitual behaviors or ongoing, stubborn problems that are hard to resolve.

- *Examples:* A chronic liar, a chronic shortage of staff, chronic traffic [[congestion][congestion]].

*** Slang Terminology
In informal or cannabis culture, *chronic* is used as a slang term to describe high-quality, potent marijuana. It was popularized by the 1992 Dr. Dre album /The Chronic/.

* congestion

"*Congestion*" descrbies *a situation where a place, system, or bodily passage is excessively blocked, overcrowded, or clogged, causing difficulty in movement or function*.

** Common uses of the term
- *Traffic & Crowding:* The blocking of roads, public transit, or public areas due to an overwhelming volume of people or vehicles (e.g., severe traffic congestion on major highways).
- *Medical Conditions:* The abnormal accumulation or blockage of fluids, mucus, or blood in a body part, such as nasal congestion (stuffy nose) or chest congestion.
- *Network & Tech:* The overloading of a digital network or telecommunications system, which can cause delays in data transmission.
