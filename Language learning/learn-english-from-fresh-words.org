* sheet

Typically a "sheet" is *a flat, broad, and thin piece of material or a broad expanse of something*.

- *Paper*: A single piece of paper for writing or printing.
- *Bedding*: A large, thin rectangular piece of cloth used as part of bed linen (like a flat or fitted sheet).
- *Material*: A thin, flat layer of a solid substance like glass, metal or ice.
- *Computing & Business*: A digital document, often called a spreadsheet (e.g. in Google Sheets or Microsoft Excel), used for storing, organizing, and calculating data in rows and columns.

** Specific terms:

- Check out a *Balance Sheet* to see a financial summary of a company's assets, liabilities and equity.
- Create and manage your own tables using *Google Sheets*.

* conversation

*An interactive, verbal or written exchange of ideas, thoughts, feelings and information between 2 or more people.* It requires active participation - both speaking and listening.

** Key Elements of a Conversation

- *Turn-taking*: Participants alternate between listening and speaking.
- *Information Exchange*: The goal is usually to convey news, solve problems or simply share experience.
- *Medium*: Face-to-face, over the phone or digitally through text and messaging apps.

** Types of Conversations

Conversations can range from casual to highly structured:

- *Small Talk*: Light, casual conversation used to break the ice or discuss impersonal topics like the wheather.
- *Discussion*: A more serious exchange of ideas to explore an issue or to make a decision.
- *Heart-to-Heart*: An intimate, personal exchange where individuals share deep feelings and trust.
- *Debate*: A highly structured, formal argument where different viewpoints systematically defined.

** Etymology

The word originates from the Latin /conversatio/, which combines /con-/ ("together") and /versare/ ("to turn"). Historically, it literally meant "turning together" and originally referred to general social behavior or how individuals lived their lives together.

* verbatim
/və(ː)ˈbeɪtɪm/

** Usage Examples:

- *Adjective*: "The lawyer provided a *verbatim* transcript of the court hearing."

** Etymology

The term drives from Medieval Latin /verbātim/, which combines the Latin word /verbum/ (meaning "word") with an adverbail suffix meaning "by the" or "in".

** Common Synonyms

- Word for word
- Exactly
- Precisely

** Common question

Q1: Is "literally" a synonyms of verbatim?
A1: No. They have different meanings and distinct uses.
- The Difference
  - *Verbatim*: Means coping or repeating words *exactly as spoken or written* (word-for-word).
  - *Literally*: Means something happened *exactly in a strict sense*, without exaggeration or metaphor.
- Examples Contrast
  - "Copy this *verbatim*." (Use the exact same words).
  - "I am *literally* freezing." (Your body temperature is actually dropping; you are not using a figure of speech).
- Cross-Over Exception
  They only overlap in very specific contexts regarding translation or interpretation:
  - A "*leteral* translation" can mean a "*verbatim* translation" (translating word-for-word instead of for overall meaning).

* introverted

Describes *a personality trait characterized by a focus on internal thoughts, feelings and mood rather than seeking external [[stimulation][stimulation]]*. Introverts typically recharge their energy through solitude and quiet environments, often finding large social gathering draining.

** The Science of Energy

In psychology - a framework popularized by Carl Jung - the distinction between introverts and extroverts comes down to how you process and regain energy.

- *Introverts* gain energy from their internal world. After engaging in heavy social interaction, they usually need to retreat to a quiet environment to mentally recharge.
- *Extroverts* gain energey from outside world and are stimulated by social interactions.

** Common Traits

Introverts often share several behavioral and mental characteristics:

- Deep Focus: They prefer concentrating on a few projects or ideas in-depth rather than multitasking across many.
- Quality Relationships: They generally favor close, intimate friendships over large social networks.
- Observation: They tend to think things through internally before speaking or taking actions.

** Common Misconceptions

- Myth: Introversion is the same as shyness or anti-social behavior. Shyness [[involve][involves]] a fear of social judgement, whereas introversion simply [[dictate][dictates]] how much social stimulation one can handle before needing a break.
- Myth: Introverts cannot be leaders or public speakers. Many introverts are highly [[charismatic][charismatic]], confident and effective leaders, though they may still prefer one-to-one meeting over crowdwd networking events.

* stimulation

*Stimulation* refers to *the act of arousing, encouraging, or exciting an individual, organism, or system to increased activity, energy, or development*.

It can refer to anything that _provokes_ a physical, mental, or emotional response.

** Sensory and Physical

The activation of nerve receptors or the senses via external factors like light, sound or touch. For instance, visual stimulation can catch a child's interest, while muscle or nerve stimulation relates to raising physiological activity in the body.

** Cognitive and Mental

Activities or experiences that challenge and engage the mind. People might read a complex book or engage in a challenging debate to get the mental or intellectual stimulation they _crave_.

** Economic and Biological

The encouragement of development or the causing of activity. In biology, it can mean promoting the function of a body part (like stimulating the _ovaries_). In economics, a government might use policies (such as a cut in interest rates) to stimulate market growth.

** Sexual

Mutual touching or fondling meant to _evoke_ sexual interest or arousal before _intercourse_.

*** 2. To state aloud for transcription
This refers to verbally speaking text so that a secretary or a digital device can _transcribe_ it.

- *Example:* Local climate will *dictate* the types of crops you can grow.

* involve

The verb "*involve*" means *to include or contain something as a necessary part, consequence, or feature*. It often refers to drawing someone into an activity, situation, or consequence (e.g., "The job involves travel" or "The accident involved two cars").

** Core Applications
The meaning of "*involve*" generally splits into three distinct applications:

- *To require or entail:* It indicates that a process necessitates a specific action or condition. For example, "Building a house involves planning and hard work." 
- *To include as a participant:* It means drawing a person or group into an activity or event. For example, "The teacher likes to involve all students in classroom discussions."
- *To relate or affect:* It shows that a person, thing, or circumstance is concerned or caught up in a situation. For example, "The scandal involved several high-ranking officials."

** Involve vs. Include
Users online often discuss the difference between *involve* and *include*. While "include" simply means listing components as part of a whole, "involve" implies a deeper, more active, or complex connection where the elements are necessary or tangled together in the activity.

* essay

An *essay* is *a short piece of non-fiction writing that presents an argument, analyzes a topic, or expresses the author's personal point of view*. Derived from the French word /essai/ (meaning "a trial" or "an attempt"), it typically features an introduction, supporting evidence, and a conclusion.

** Key Types of Essays
- *Expository:* Requires the writer to investigate an idea, evaluate evidence, and present the idea clearly.
- *Persuasive (Argumentative):* Makes a specific claim about a topic and uses evidence and logic to convince the reader.
- *Narrative:* Tells a story, often drawn from personal experience, to make a broader point.
- *Descriptive:* Focuses on painting a picture of a person, place, or object using _sensory_ details.

** Historical Context
The concept was pioneered by the French writer *Michel de Montaigne* in the 16th century. He coined the term to describe his writings as "attempts" to put his thoughts and reflections into words. 

** Usage Examples
- *Academic:* "The students were required to submit a 1,000-word *essay* on the French Revolution."
- *Literary:* "She published a brilliant *essay* exploring the themes of _mortality_ in modern poetry."
- *Verb (Formal/ _Archaic_):* "He decided to *essay* a challenging climb up the north face of the mountain."

* patent

A "*patent*" is *an intellectual property right granted by a government to an inventor*. It gives the owner the exclusive legal right to exclude others from making, using, selling, or importing the invention for a limited period, typically 20 years, in exchange for publicly disclosing how the invention works.

** Key Concepts
*** The Quid Pro Quo
A patent is essentially a bargain between the inventor and the public.
- *The Inventor's Benefit:* Receives a temporary monopoly to profit from their work.
- *The Public's Benefit:* Detailed blueprints and instructions are published.
- *The Goal:* Ensures that once the patent expires, the technology enters the public domain, allowing others to freely use and improve upon it.

*** Types of Patents
Patents are generally categorized into three main types:
1. *Utility Patents:* Protect how a product functions, works, or is manufactured (e.g., new machinery, chemical formulas, or software).
2. *Design Patents:* Protect the aesthetic or ornamental appearance of a functional item (e.g., the shape of a smartphone or a unique shoe design).
3. *Plant Patents:* Safeguard new, asexually reproduced plant varieties.

** Requirements for Patentability
Not all ideas can be patented. To qualify for a utility patent, an invention must meet three strict criteria:

- *Novel:* It must be entirely new and not previously known or published anywhere in the world.
- *Useful:* It must have a practical application and actually perform its intended purpose.
- *Non-obvious:* It must involve a creative step that would not be obvious to someone with ordinary skill in that particular field of technology.

** Related Terminology
- *Patent Pending:* A phrase used by inventors or companies while a patent application is actively under review. It serves as a warning to competitors that a patent may soon be granted.
- *Infringement:* The unauthorized making, using, or selling of a patented invention during the life of the patent. Patent holders can take legal action to stop this and seek damages.
- *Patent Troll (Non-Practicing Entity):* A colloquial term for a person or company that buys the rights to a patent with the sole intent of suing others for infringement, rather than actually manufacturing or developing the product themselves.

* amend

*Amend* is a formal verb that means to *change, modify, or improve a piece of writing, a statement, or a law*. The goal of amending something is usually to correct a mistake, update information, or make the original text more accurate.

*** Core Definitions
   - *To alter a legal document*: Changing the wording of laws, contracts, or constitutions through a formal process.
   - *To correct or improve*: Modifying written or spoken text to remove faults.
   - *To improve behavior*: An older or more formal usage meaning to reform one's own conduct ("to amend one's ways").

*** Common Examples
   - *Law*: "The country's constitution was *amended* to allow women to vote."
   - *Business*: "The terms of the contract were *amended* to extend the deadline."
   - *Speech*: "He said he stole the money - or rather, borrowed it, he *amended*."

*** "Amend" vs. "Emend"
   While they sound similar and both mean "to change for the better," they are used in different contexts:
   - *Amend (with an 'A')*: The general term used for changing laws, contracts, plans, or behaviors.
   - *Emend (with an 'E')*: A very specific term used almost exclusively for editing or correcting typos and errors in a text or manuscript.

*** Idiom: "To Make Amends"
   When turned into the plural noun phrase *make amends*, the meaning shifts to *actively repairing a relationship or correcting a mistake* after causing harm. It goes beyond a simple apology to include actions that fix the bad situation.
   - *Example*: "He tried to *make amends* for his rudeness by buying her flowers."

* gender

"*Gender*" refers to *the socially constructed roles, behaviors, expressions, and identities of girls, women, boys, men, and gender-diverse people*. It determines how we are perceived and expected to act, influencing our opportunities and societal power dynamics.

** Gender vs. Sex
While *sex* categorizes people by biological traits (like chromosomes and reproductive organs), *gender* relates to the behavioral, cultural, and psychological traits associated with those categories.

** Key Dimensions of Gender
To fully understand the meaning of gender, it is often broken down into three related concepts:

- *Gender Identity:* A person's deeply felt, internal sense of being male, female, a combination of both, or neither. This may or may not align with the sex they were assigned at birth.
- *Gender Expression:* The way a person publicly presents their gender in society through behavior, clothing, hairstyles, voice, or body characteristics.
- *Gender Roles:* The societal norms, tasks, and behaviors that a culture deems appropriate for men, women, or other identities. Because these are social constructs, they change over time and vary across cultures.

** Other Contexts
Beyond sociology and psychology, the word "*gender*" is used in other fields:

- *Grammar:* In languages like Spanish or French, gender refers to the classification of nouns and pronouns into categories such as masculine, feminine, or neuter.

* contract

A "*contract*" is *a legally binding agreement between two or more parties that outlines specific rights, duties, and obligations*. It typically involves an exchange of value (like goods, services, or money) and provides legal remedies if the terms are _breached_.

** Key Elements of a Valid Contract
To be legally enforceable, an agreement usually requires the following core components:
*** Offer
A clear proposal to enter into a specific deal.
*** Acceptance
Unconditional agreement to the exact terms of the offer.
*** Consideration
Something of value exchanged between the parties (e.g., money, services, or a promise not to do something).
*** Intent
Both parties must intend for the agreement to be recognized by law.
*** Capacity
Parties must be of legal age, sober, and mentally competent.
*** Legality
The contract must be for a lawful purpose.

** Types of Contracts
- Express Contract :: The terms are clearly stated, either verbally or in writing.
- Implied Contract :: The agreement is formed by the actions and conduct of the parties rather than their explicit words (e.g., ordering food at a restaurant implies you will pay for it).
- Unilateral Contract :: One party makes a promise in exchange for a specific action from the other party (e.g., a reward for a lost pet).
- Bilateral Contract :: Both parties exchange promises to perform (e.g., a standard employment agreement).

** What Happens if a Contract is Broken?
If one party fails to fulfill their obligations without a legal excuse, it is known as a *breach of contract*. The injured party can pursue legal remedies, which may include:
1. *Damages*: Financial compensation for the losses suffered.
2. *Specific Performance*: A court order requiring the breaching party to complete the task they originally promised.
3. *Rescission*: Canceling the contract entirely and returning both parties to their pre-contract state.

   1. *a supporter of policies that are socially progressive and promote social welfare*.
      - "she dissented from the decision, joined by the court's liberals"
   2. *a supporter of a political and social philosophy that promotes individual rights, civil liberties, democracy, and free enterprise*.
   3. *a supporter or member of a Liberal party or (in the UK) the Liberal Democrat Party*.

** Etymology
*** Derivation Flow
    - LATIN: *liber* (free (man)) -> LATIN: *liberalis* \
      => OLD FRENCH \
      => ENGLISH: *liberal arts* -> ENGLISH: *liberal* (Middle English)

*** Etymology Note
    Middle English: via Old French from Latin /liberalis/, from /liber/ 'free (man)'. The original sense was 'suitable for a free man', hence 'suitable for a gentleman' (one not tied to a trade), surviving in /liberal arts/. Another early sense 'generous' (compare with *liberal* (sense 4 of the adjective)) gave rise to an obsolete meaning 'free from restraint', leading to *liberal* (sense 1 of the adjective) (late 18th century).

* cognitive

"*Cognitive*" relates to *the mental processes involved in acquiring knowledge, processing information, and understanding the world*. It encompasses conscious and unconscious activities like thinking, memory, learning, perception, attention, and decision-making.

** Core Fields of Study
*** Cognitive Psychology
This branch of psychology examines internal mental states, focusing on how people perceive, process, and store information. It explores how we interpret our senses, form memories, and solve problems.

*** Cognitive Development
The study of how a person's intellectual abilities and thought processes change as they grow, notably theorized by developmental psychologists like Jean Piaget.

*** Cognitive Function
A term often used in healthcare and neurology to describe an individual's mental capabilities, such as reasoning, focus, and language skills.

** Practical Application
In practical terms, whenever your brain is working to interpret a sight, learn a new skill, focus on a conversation, or make a choice, it is engaging in a *cognitive process*.

* neglect
"*Neglect*" means *failing to give enough care, attention, or effort to someone or something, usually due to carelessness or a lapse in responsibility*. It can function as both a verb (the action of ignoring or failing to do something) and a noun (the resulting state of being uncared for).

** Key Usages and Forms
*** Verb (to neglect)
To fail to look after someone or something, or to forget to do a required task.
- /Example:/ "He *neglected* his garden, and it became overgrown."
- /Example:/ "She *neglected* to lock the front door."

*** Verb (neglect to do)
To fail to do something, often accidentally or due to forgetfulness.
- /Example:/ "I *neglected to* inform them of the change in schedule."

*** Noun (neglect)
The state of being ignored or the act of leaving something in disrepair.
- /Example:/ "The old building collapsed after years of *neglect*."

** Related Contexts
- *Legal and Medical:* In caregiving and legal contexts, neglect is a severe issue where someone responsible for a dependent fails to provide basic necessities (like food, medical care, or supervision).
- *Common Synonyms:* Disregard, ignore, overlook, abandon, or slack on.

* continent

A "*continent*" is *a massive, continuous expanse of land on Earth's surface, generally separated by vast oceans or distinct physical boundaries*.

** The Seven Continents
The Earth is traditionally divided into seven continents, ordered from largest to smallest by land area:

*** Asia
The largest continent, covering about 30% of the Earth's total land area and home to over half of the world's population.

*** Africa
The second-largest, known for its incredible geographical and cultural diversity, and home to the world's longest river (the Nile) and the largest hot desert (the Sahara).

*** North America
The third-largest continent, containing Canada, the United States, Mexico, Central American countries, and the Caribbean islands.

*** South America
The fourth-largest continent, primarily known for the Amazon River and Rainforest, and the Andes mountain range.

*** Antarctica
The fifth-largest and least-populated continent. It is almost entirely covered by a massive ice sheet and is primarily used for scientific research.

*** Europe
The sixth-largest continent, characterized by a long, heavily _indented_ coastline and a rich history of distinct yet deeply intertwined cultures.

*** Australia (or Oceania)
The smallest continent. It is completely surrounded by water and consists of mainland Australia, Tasmania, and numerous surrounding islands.

** Key Characteristics of Continents
- *Geological Foundation:* Continents are primarily made of thick, _buoyant_, lower-density rock called continental _crust_. This crust floats atop the Earth's semi-fluid _mantle_ and rests on massive _tectonic_ _plates_.
- *Plate Tectonics:* Because they sit on tectonic plates, continents are not stationary. Over hundreds of millions of years, they slowly shift, collide, and separate - a process that once formed the supercontinent Pangea. 
- *Continental Shelf:* The boundary of a continent does not simply end at the beach. It extends out into the ocean on a gently sloping area of land known as a continental shelf before dropping off into deep ocean water.

** Additional Notes
Depending on cultural and geographical conventions, some regions like Europe and Asia are sometimes grouped together and referred to collectively as *Eurasia*.

* incentive

An "*incentive*" is anything that *motivates, encourages, or drives a person or organization to take a specific action or alter their behavior*. Usually offered in the form of a reward, incentives are designed to increase effort and align personal interests with desired outcomes.

Incentives play a major role in both daily life and the economy, and they are generally categorized into the following types:

** Types of Incentives
*** Financial/Monetary
Tangible monetary rewards such as bonuses, cash prizes, or discount codes designed to persuade you to buy a product or work harder at your job.

*** Fiscal/Regulatory
Broad policies like tax breaks that a government uses to encourage specific public behaviors, such as investing in renewable energy or buying an electric vehicle.

*** Intrinsic/Non-monetary
Non-financial motivators that appeal to emotions or well-being, such as receiving a flexible work schedule, public recognition, or personal praise.

*** Disincentives
The opposite of an incentive; a penalty or a cost designed to discourage a specific action (e.g., parking fines or higher taxes on cigarettes).

** Common Examples
*** In the Workplace
Employers often use commission structures, profit-sharing, or performance bonuses as an *incentive* to boost productivity and employee retention.

*** In Marketing
Retailers offer promotions like "buy one, get one free" or limited-time rebates as an *incentive* for shoppers to make a purchase.

- *Leisure industry* :: The business sector that provides entertainment, travel, and recreational services.

* implication

An "*implication*" is *a conclusion, consequence, or suggestion that is drawn from a situation but not directly stated*. It represents the indirect effect an action will have, or an unstated hint.

** Core Dimensions of Implication

*** 1. Future Consequences
In decision-making, an implication is the likely outcome or downstream result of an action.
- *Example:* "We must consider the environmental implications of building the new highway."

*** 2. Suggested Meaning (Subtext)
An implication can be a nuance, hint, or subtext. The speaker /implies/, and the listener /infers/.
- *Example:* "When she said I should 'update my resume,' the implication was that my job might be at risk."

*** 3. Legal/Behavioral Involvement
In a negative context, it refers to someone’s involvement or entanglement in a crime or bad situation.
- *Example:* "The investigation revealed his implication in the fraudulent scheme."

*** 4. Logical Implication
In mathematics and formal reasoning, it is a conditional "if-then" relationship.
- *Formula:* If A is true, then B must be true ($A \implies B$).

* retail
"*Retail*" is *the process of selling goods or services directly to individual consumers for personal use*. As the final step in the supply chain, retailers buy products in large quantities from manufacturers or wholesalers and sell them in smaller amounts to the public, typically at a marked-up price for profit.

** The Supply Chain
Understanding retail involves looking at where it sits in the broader product lifecycle:
- *Manufacturer:* Creates the product.
- *Wholesaler:* Purchases large batches from manufacturers and distributes them to retailers.
- *Retailer:* Sells the final product directly to the end consumer.
- *Consumer:* The end user who buys the product for personal or household use.

** Common Types of Retail
Retailing takes place across several different formats, both online and offline:
- *Brick-and-Mortar:* Physical storefronts where customers can browse and purchase items in person, such as supermarkets, department stores, and specialty shops.
- *E-commerce:* Digital marketplaces and online stores that allow consumers to buy products online and have them delivered.
- *Mobile Retail:* Purchases made directly via smartphone apps or mobile websites.
- *Services:* Retail extends beyond physical goods; it also includes consumer services such as hair salons, travel agencies, and restaurants.

** Why Retail Matters
For businesses, retail involves much more than just a single point of sale. It encompasses marketing, merchandising, pricing strategy, inventory management, and customer service. It is a massive economic driver that not only creates millions of jobs but also shapes consumer trends and daily lifestyle habits.

* conscious

** Primary Contexts
*** State of Being (Awake)
Being conscious means having your mental faculties active and not dulled by sleep, anesthesia, or fainting.
- *Example:* "The patient remained *conscious* during the entire medical procedure."

*** Awareness and Perception
It means noticing, sensing, or realizing that something exists or is occurring.
- *Example:* "I was *conscious* of someone staring at me in the coffee shop."

*** Intentionality
When used as "conscious effort" or "conscious decision," it means an action done with full awareness, deliberation, and purpose.

It is often attached to nouns to indicate someone who is highly concerned or preoccupied with a specific topic.

People often mix *conscious* with *conscience*. 
- *Conscious:* Refers to your physical and mental *awareness*.

* resume
A "*resume*" (or /résumé/) is *a concise document that summarizes a job applicant’s work experience, education, and skills*. Primarily used to apply for employment, its purpose is to market your qualifications and secure a job interview.

The word "resume" has two primary definitions and pronunciations depending on how it is used:

** 1. As a Noun (Pronounced /REZ-oo-may/)
This is the job application document. A strong resume acts as a professional marketing tool rather than a comprehensive autobiography, and it is usually kept to one or two pages. It typically includes:

- *Contact Information:* Name, phone number, email, and LinkedIn profile.
- *Professional Summary:* A brief statement of your goals and qualifications.
- *Work History:* Past job titles, companies, employment dates, and key achievements.
- *Education:* Degrees, certifications, and institutions.
- *Skills:* Specific abilities relevant to the job you are applying for.

For a broader summary of your career, similar documents are referred to as a *Curriculum Vitae (CV)*. To ensure your resume stands out, it should be tailored with relevant keywords to fit each specific job you apply for.

** 2. As a Verb (Pronounced /ri-ZOOM/)
This means to begin again or restart an activity after an interruption.

For exmaple: 
- "The meeting will /resume/ after lunch."
- "The game /resumed/ after the rain delay."

* infant

An "*infant*" is *a very young human, typically under one year of age*. The term broadly describes a baby from birth until they begin walking and transitioning into the "toddler" stage.

** Contextual Meanings
*** Age Classifications (Medical & Developmental)
- *Newborn (Neonate):* Applies to the first 28 days of life.
- *Infant:* Generally refers to children from 1 month to 1 year old.
- *Toddler:* The stage following infancy, typically between 1 and 3 years old.

*** Legal Definition
In the legal context, an "infant" is synonymous with a minor. It refers to anyone who has not yet reached the age of majority (usually 18 years old).

* estate

An "*estate*" generally refers to *the entirety of a person's wealth, including all property, assets, and liabilities*. Depending on the context, it can also refer to a large, exclusive plot of land with a substantial residence, or a planned, multi-unit housing or industrial development.

** Key Definitions by Context

*** Law and Inheritance
An estate comprises all the physical and intangible assets a person owns upon their death minus any outstanding debts.
- Examples: Real estate, bank accounts, investments, personal items.
- Role: An "executor" manages the distribution of this estate.

*** Property and Real Estate
This describes a large country property featuring a grand house, gardens, and grounds.
- Note: The term "real estate" specifically refers to land and the permanent buildings attached to it.

*** Housing Developments
In British English, an estate often refers to a residential area built with a planned, uniform layout by a single developer.
- Synonyms: Housing estate, housing scheme.

** Related Legal & Financial Terms

*** Estate Planning
The process of anticipating and arranging, during a person's life, for the management and disposal of their estate during their life, in the event of incapacity, or upon death.

*** Estate Tax
A tax levied on an heir's inherited portion of an estate, usually triggered only if the estate's total value exceeds a specific government threshold.

*** Estate Agent
(UK) A professional who represents buyers and sellers of real property (similar to a *real estate agent* or *realtor* in the US).
