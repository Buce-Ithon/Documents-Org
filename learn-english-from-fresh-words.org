* sheet

Typically a "sheet" is *a flat, broad, and thin piece of material or a broad expanse of something*.

- *Paper*: A single piece of paper for writing or printing.
- *Bedding*: A large, thin rectangular piece of cloth used as part of bed linen (like a flat or fitted sheet).
- *Material*: A thin, flat layer of a solid substance like glass, metal or ice.
- *Computing & Business*: A digital document, often called a spreadsheet (e.g. in Google Sheets or Microsoft Excel), used for storing, organizing, and calculating data in rows and columns.

** Specific terms:

- Check out a *Balance Sheet* to see a financial summary of a company's assets, liabilities and equity.
- Create and manage your own tables using *Google Sheets*.

* conversation

*An interactive, verbal or written exchange of ideas, thoughts, feelings and information between 2 or more people.* It requires active participation - both speaking and listening.

** Key Elements of a Conversation

- *Turn-taking*: Participants alternate between listening and speaking.
- *Information Exchange*: The goal is usually to convey news, solve problems or simply share experience.
- *Medium*: Face-to-face, over the phone or digitally through text and messaging apps.

** Types of Conversations

Conversations can range from casual to highly structured:

- *Small Talk*: Light, casual conversation used to break the ice or discuss impersonal topics like the wheather.
- *Discussion*: A more serious exchange of ideas to explore an issue or to make a decision.
- *Heart-to-Heart*: An intimate, personal exchange where individuals share deep feelings and trust.
- *Debate*: A highly structured, formal argument where different viewpoints systematically defined.

** Etymology

The word originates from the Latin /conversatio/, which combines /con-/ ("together") and /versare/ ("to turn"). Historically, it literally meant "turning together" and originally referred to general social behavior or how individuals lived their lives together.

* verbatim
/və(ː)ˈbeɪtɪm/

*Word-for-word, using exactly the same words as were originally spoken or written.* It ensures the information is reproduced precisely with no alterations.

** Usage Examples:

- *Adjective*: "The lawyer provided a *verbatim* transcript of the court hearing."

** Etymology

The term drives from Medieval Latin /verbātim/, which combines the Latin word /verbum/ (meaning "word") with an adverbail suffix meaning "by the" or "in".

** Common Synonyms

- Word for word
- Exactly
- Precisely
- Directly

** Common question

Q1: Is "literally" a synonyms of verbatim?
A1: No. They have different meanings and distinct uses.
- The Difference
  - *Verbatim*: Means coping or repeating words *exactly as spoken or written* (word-for-word).
  - *Literally*: Means something happened *exactly in a strict sense*, without exaggeration or metaphor.
- Examples Contrast
  - "Copy this *verbatim*." (Use the exact same words).
  - "I am *literally* freezing." (Your body temperature is actually dropping; you are not using a figure of speech).
- Cross-Over Exception
  They only overlap in very specific contexts regarding translation or interpretation:
  - A "*leteral* translation" can mean a "*verbatim* translation" (translating word-for-word instead of for overall meaning).

* introverted

Describes *a personality trait characterized by a focus on internal thoughts, feelings and mood rather than seeking eternal [[stimulation][stimulation]]*. Introverts typically recharge their energy through solitude and quiet environments, often finding large social gathering draining.

** The Science of Energy

In psychology - a framework popularized by Carl Jung - the distinction between introverts and extroverts comes down to how you process and regain energy.

- *Introverts* gain energy from their internal world. After engaging in heavy social interaction, they usually need to retreat to a quiet environment to mentally recharge.
- *Extroverts* gain energey from outside world and are stimulated by social interactions.

** Common Traits

Introverts often share several behavioral and mental characteristics:

- Deep Focus: They prefer concentrating on a few projects or ideas in-depth rather than multitasking across many.
- Quality Relationships: They generally favor close, intimate friendships over large social networks.
- Observation: They tend to think things through internally before speaking or taking actions.

** Common Misconceptions

- Myth: Introversion is the same as shyness or anti-social behavior. Shyness [[involve][involves]] a fear of social judgement, whereas introversion simply [[dictate][dictates]] how much social stimulation one can handle before needing a break.
- Myth: Introverts cannot be leaders or public speakers. Many introverts are highly [[charismatic][charismatic]], confident and effective leaders, though they may still prefer one-to-one meeting over crowdwd networking events.

* stimulation

*Stimulation* refers to *the act of arousing, encouraging, or exciting an individual, organism, or system to increased activity, energy, or development*.

It can refer to anything that _provokes_ a physical, mental, or emotional response.

** Sensory and Physical

The activation of nerve receptors or the senses via external factors like light, sound or touch. For instance, visual stimulation can catch a child's interest, while muscle or nerve stimulation relates to raising physiological activity in the body.

** Cognitive and Mental

Activities or experiences that challenge and engage the mind. People might read a complex book or engage in a challenging debate to get the mental or intellectual stimulation they _crave_.

** Economic and Biological

The encouragement of development or the causing of activity. In biology, it can mean promoting the function of a body part (like stimulating the _ovaries_). In economics, a government might use policies (such as a cut in interest rates) to stimulate market growth.

** Sexual

Mutual touching or fondling meant to _evoke_ sexual interest or arousal before _intercourse_.

* involve

The verb "*involve*" means *to include or contain something as a necessary part, consequence, or feature*. It often refers to drawing someone into an activity, situation, or consequence (e.g., "The job involves travel" or "The accident involved two cars").

** Core Applications
The meaning of "*involve*" generally splits into three distinct applications:

- *To require or entail:* It indicates that a process necessitates a specific action or condition. For example, "Building a house involves planning and hard work." 
- *To include as a participant:* It means drawing a person or group into an activity or event. For example, "The teacher likes to involve all students in classroom discussions."
- *To relate or affect:* It shows that a person, thing, or circumstance is concerned or caught up in a situation. For example, "The scandal involved several high-ranking officials."

** Involve vs. Include
Users online often discuss the difference between *involve* and *include*. While "include" simply means listing components as part of a whole, "involve" implies a deeper, more active, or complex connection where the elements are necessary or tangled together in the activity.

* essay

An *essay* is *a short piece of non-fiction writing that presents an argument, analyzes a topic, or expresses the author's personal point of view*. Derived from the French word /essai/ (meaning "a trial" or "an attempt"), it typically features an introduction, supporting evidence, and a conclusion.

** Key Types of Essays
- *Expository:* Requires the writer to investigate an idea, evaluate evidence, and present the idea clearly.
- *Persuasive (Argumentative):* Makes a specific claim about a topic and uses evidence and logic to convince the reader.
- *Narrative:* Tells a story, often drawn from personal experience, to make a broader point.
- *Descriptive:* Focuses on painting a picture of a person, place, or object using _sensory_ details.

** Historical Context
The concept was pioneered by the French writer *Michel de Montaigne* in the 16th century. He coined the term to describe his writings as "attempts" to put his thoughts and reflections into words. 

** Usage Examples
- *Academic:* "The students were required to submit a 1,000-word *essay* on the French Revolution."
- *Literary:* "She published a brilliant *essay* exploring the themes of _mortality_ in modern poetry."
- *Verb (Formal/ _Archaic_):* "He decided to *essay* a challenging climb up the north face of the mountain."

* patent

A "*patent*" is *an intellectual property right granted by a government to an inventor*. It gives the owner the exclusive legal right to exclude others from making, using, selling, or importing the invention for a limited period, typically 20 years, in exchange for publicly disclosing how the invention works.

** Key Concepts
*** The Quid Pro Quo
A patent is essentially a bargain between the inventor and the public.
- *The Inventor's Benefit:* Receives a temporary monopoly to profit from their work.
- *The Public's Benefit:* Detailed blueprints and instructions are published.
- *The Goal:* Ensures that once the patent expires, the technology enters the public domain, allowing others to freely use and improve upon it.

*** Types of Patents
Patents are generally categorized into three main types:
1. *Utility Patents:* Protect how a product functions, works, or is manufactured (e.g., new machinery, chemical formulas, or software).
2. *Design Patents:* Protect the aesthetic or ornamental appearance of a functional item (e.g., the shape of a smartphone or a unique shoe design).
3. *Plant Patents:* Safeguard new, asexually reproduced plant varieties.

** Requirements for Patentability
Not all ideas can be patented. To qualify for a utility patent, an invention must meet three strict criteria:

- *Novel:* It must be entirely new and not previously known or published anywhere in the world.
- *Useful:* It must have a practical application and actually perform its intended purpose.
- *Non-obvious:* It must involve a creative step that would not be obvious to someone with ordinary skill in that particular field of technology.

** Related Terminology
- *Patent Pending:* A phrase used by inventors or companies while a patent application is actively under review. It serves as a warning to competitors that a patent may soon be granted.
- *Infringement:* The unauthorized making, using, or selling of a patented invention during the life of the patent. Patent holders can take legal action to stop this and seek damages.
- *Patent Troll (Non-Practicing Entity):* A colloquial term for a person or company that buys the rights to a patent with the sole intent of suing others for infringement, rather than actually manufacturing or developing the product themselves.

* amend

*Amend* is a formal verb that means to *change, modify, or improve a piece of writing, a statement, or a law*. The goal of amending something is usually to correct a mistake, update information, or make the original text more accurate.

*** Core Definitions
   - *To alter a legal document*: Changing the wording of laws, contracts, or constitutions through a formal process.
   - *To correct or improve*: Modifying written or spoken text to remove faults.
   - *To improve behavior*: An older or more formal usage meaning to reform one's own conduct ("to amend one's ways").

*** Common Examples
   - *Law*: "The country's constitution was *amended* to allow women to vote."
   - *Business*: "The terms of the contract were *amended* to extend the deadline."
   - *Speech*: "He said he stole the money - or rather, borrowed it, he *amended*."

*** "Amend" vs. "Emend"
   While they sound similar and both mean "to change for the better," they are used in different contexts:
   - *Amend (with an 'A')*: The general term used for changing laws, contracts, plans, or behaviors.
   - *Emend (with an 'E')*: A very specific term used almost exclusively for editing or correcting typos and errors in a text or manuscript.

*** Idiom: "To Make Amends"
   When turned into the plural noun phrase *make amends*, the meaning shifts to *actively repairing a relationship or correcting a mistake* after causing harm. It goes beyond a simple apology to include actions that fix the bad situation.
   - *Example*: "He tried to *make amends* for his rudeness by buying her flowers."

* contract

A "*contract*" is *a legally binding agreement between two or more parties that outlines specific rights, duties, and obligations*. It typically involves an exchange of value (like goods, services, or money) and provides legal remedies if the terms are _breached_.

** Key Elements of a Valid Contract
To be legally enforceable, an agreement usually requires the following core components:
*** Offer
A clear proposal to enter into a specific deal.
*** Acceptance
Unconditional agreement to the exact terms of the offer.
*** Consideration
Something of value exchanged between the parties (e.g., money, services, or a promise not to do something).
*** Intent
Both parties must intend for the agreement to be recognized by law.
*** Capacity
Parties must be of legal age, sober, and mentally competent.
*** Legality
The contract must be for a lawful purpose.

** Types of Contracts
- Express Contract :: The terms are clearly stated, either verbally or in writing.
- Implied Contract :: The agreement is formed by the actions and conduct of the parties rather than their explicit words (e.g., ordering food at a restaurant implies you will pay for it).
- Unilateral Contract :: One party makes a promise in exchange for a specific action from the other party (e.g., a reward for a lost pet).
- Bilateral Contract :: Both parties exchange promises to perform (e.g., a standard employment agreement).

** What Happens if a Contract is Broken?
If one party fails to fulfill their obligations without a legal excuse, it is known as a *breach of contract*. The injured party can pursue legal remedies, which may include:
1. *Damages*: Financial compensation for the losses suffered.
2. *Specific Performance*: A court order requiring the breaching party to complete the task they originally promised.
3. *Rescission*: Canceling the contract entirely and returning both parties to their pre-contract state.
